\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Address Resolution Protocol (ARP) poisoning is one of the most prevalent
Man-in-the-Middle (MitM) attack vectors in enterprise Wireless Local Area Networks
(WLANs), enabling attackers to silently intercept, alter, or drop network traffic
between legitimate hosts. Traditional rule-based and signature-driven ARP protection
mechanisms fail against sophisticated, low-rate poisoning attempts that evade static
detection thresholds. This proposal presents a machine learning approach to ARP
poisoning detection using Gradient Boosting Machines (GBM), leveraging network
flow-level features extracted from enterprise WLAN traffic. Using the CIC-IDS2017
and a custom-generated ARP poisoning dataset captured in a controlled lab environment,
the study trains and evaluates XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost classifiers, comparing
their detection accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, and false positive rate against
baseline methods including Random Forest and SVM. The proposed GBM-based detection
framework aims to achieve a detection accuracy exceeding 98\% and a false positive rate
below 2\%, providing a practical, deployable solution for enterprise network
administrators to detect MitM ARP poisoning attacks in real time.
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Problem Statement}

\textbf{Ideal State.}
In an ideal enterprise network environment, all ARP transactions between hosts
are legitimate, verified, and traceable. Network administrators should be able
to detect and block malicious ARP replies in real time, preventing any unauthorized
host from masquerading as another device on the local network segment. Intrusion
detection systems should reliably distinguish between normal ARP traffic and
poisoning attempts with high accuracy and minimal false alarms.

\textbf{Reality.}
ARP is a stateless, unauthenticated protocol by design --- any host on a Local
Area Network (LAN) can broadcast a gratuitous ARP reply claiming any IP-to-MAC
mapping, and other hosts will accept and cache it without verification \cite{tripathi2022}.
Existing defenses such as Dynamic ARP Inspection (DAI) require managed switch
infrastructure that many organizations lack, and static ARP tables are impractical
to maintain at enterprise scale. Machine learning-based detection approaches exist
but are predominantly evaluated on outdated datasets and do not specifically target
wireless enterprise environments where ARP poisoning is particularly damaging due
to shared broadcast domains \cite{ahmed2023}.

\textbf{Consequence.}
Undetected ARP poisoning attacks allow adversaries to silently intercept all
traffic between a victim and the gateway, enabling credential theft, session
hijacking, and data exfiltration. In enterprise environments, a single successful
MitM attack can expose sensitive corporate data, violate regulatory compliance
requirements, and cause financial and reputational damage estimated at millions
of dollars per incident \cite{alotaibi2023}.

\subsection{Research Objectives}

\textbf{General Objective.}
To design, implement, and evaluate a Gradient Boosting Machine (GBM)-based
detection framework that accurately identifies Man-in-the-Middle ARP poisoning
attacks in enterprise WLAN environments using network flow-level features.

\textbf{Specific SMART Objectives.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{SO\arabic*.}, leftmargin=*, itemsep=4pt]
    \item \textbf{Identify and extract} a minimum of 20 discriminative network
          flow-level features from ARP traffic (e.g., ARP reply rate, MAC-IP
          mapping consistency, broadcast ratio) using the CIC-IDS2017 dataset
          and a custom-captured WLAN dataset by \textit{Week~5} of the project
          timeline.
    \item \textbf{Train and compare} three GBM classifiers --- XGBoost, LightGBM,
          and CatBoost --- against Random Forest and SVM baseline models, achieving
          a detection accuracy of $\geq 98\%$ and a false positive rate of
          $\leq 2\%$ on the test set by \textit{Week~10}.
    \item \textbf{Evaluate and document} the best-performing model's robustness
          across low-rate and high-rate ARP poisoning scenarios and produce a
          fully reproducible open-source implementation with experimental results
          published on GitHub by \textit{Week~14}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Synthesis and Research Gap}

ARP poisoning has been recognized as a critical LAN-layer threat for over two
decades, yet the detection literature reveals a persistent gap between available
methods and practical enterprise WLAN deployments. Tripathi and Hubballi
\cite{tripathi2022} proposed an entropy-based statistical detection mechanism
that monitors deviations in ARP reply rates; while effective against high-rate
flooding attacks, the method relies on fixed thresholds that are trivially evaded
by a patient attacker operating below the detection boundary. This limitation
motivates the adoption of adaptive, data-driven approaches.

Ahmed et al.\ \cite{ahmed2023} demonstrated that Random Forest classifiers can
achieve strong precision and recall on the CIC-IDS2017 benchmark for general
MitM detection. However, their evaluation does not isolate ARP poisoning as a
distinct attack category, and the dataset was not captured in a wireless
environment where broadcast domain characteristics significantly alter traffic
patterns. Alotaibi et al.\ \cite{alotaibi2023} applied Convolutional Neural
Networks to ARP detection and reported high AUC values, but the computational
cost and lack of feature interpretability make deployment in resource-constrained
enterprise environments impractical.

On the boosting side, Stiawan et al.\ \cite{stiawan2024} confirmed that XGBoost
outperforms traditional classifiers on network intrusion detection tasks using
UNSW-NB15, but their work is not ARP-specific and ignores the unique challenges
of wireless shared broadcast domains. Rajendran et al.\ \cite{rajendran2022}
proposed a hybrid SVM and signature-based approach for enterprise MitM detection,
which still depends on signature database maintenance and does not generalize to
novel ARP poisoning variants.

The synthesis reveals a clear and actionable gap: \textit{no existing study
applies and compares multiple Gradient Boosting Machine variants (XGBoost,
LightGBM, CatBoost) specifically for ARP poisoning detection in enterprise
WLAN environments, with evaluation across both low-rate and high-rate attack
scenarios}. This proposal directly addresses that gap.

\section{Technical Methodology and Experimental Design}

\subsection{System Architecture}

The proposed detection framework follows a five-stage pipeline:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{Stage~\arabic*:}, leftmargin=*, itemsep=4pt]
    \item \textbf{Traffic Capture and Labeling.}
          ARP traffic is captured from a controlled enterprise WLAN testbed using
          Wireshark and Scapy. Attack traffic is generated using Ettercap and
          ARPspoof tools under both low-rate (1--5 packets/sec) and high-rate
          ($>$100 packets/sec) poisoning scenarios. Each packet flow is labeled
          as \textit{normal} or \textit{ARP poisoning}.

    \item \textbf{Feature Extraction.}
          Twenty flow-level features are extracted per ARP session, including:
          ARP reply-to-request ratio, MAC-IP mapping consistency score,
          gratuitous ARP frequency, sender MAC change rate, broadcast packet
          ratio, inter-arrival time statistics (mean, std, min, max), and
          session duration. Feature extraction is implemented using PyShark
          and Pandas.

    \item \textbf{Preprocessing.}
          Raw features are cleaned (null removal, outlier capping at the 99th
          percentile), normalized using Min-Max scaling, and split into 70\%
          training, 15\% validation, and 15\% test sets using stratified
          sampling to preserve class balance.

    \item \textbf{Model Training and Comparison.}
          Three GBM classifiers --- XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost --- are
          trained and hyperparameter-tuned using 5-fold cross-validation.
          Baseline models (Random Forest, SVM) are trained under identical
          conditions for fair comparison.

    \item \textbf{Evaluation.}
          All models are evaluated on the held-out test set using accuracy,
          precision, recall, F1-score, false positive rate, and AUC-ROC.
          Feature importance is analyzed using SHAP values to provide
          interpretability for network administrators.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Tool Justification}

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=4pt]
    \item \textbf{Python 3.11} --- Industry-standard for ML research with
          extensive networking and data processing libraries.
    \item \textbf{XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost} --- The three leading GBM
          implementations. Chosen over deep learning (CNN, LSTM) because GBMs
          offer superior performance on structured tabular network flow data,
          require significantly less training data, and provide interpretable
          feature importance --- critical for security deployments.
    \item \textbf{Scikit-learn 1.4} --- Used for baseline models (Random Forest,
          SVM), preprocessing pipelines, and cross-validation utilities.
    \item \textbf{Scapy + PyShark} --- Packet-level feature extraction from
          PCAP files. Scapy is chosen over dpkt for its active maintenance
          and richer ARP protocol support.
    \item \textbf{SHAP} --- Model explainability library; provides per-feature
          contribution scores essential for security analyst trust in the model.
    \item \textbf{Wireshark + Ettercap} --- Industry-standard tools for traffic
          capture and ARP poisoning attack simulation respectively.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Data Strategy}

\textbf{Datasets:}
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{CIC-IDS2017} (Canadian Institute for Cybersecurity, IEEE DataPort)
          --- 2.8 million records covering multiple attack types including MitM.
          Used to validate the GBM framework on a widely-cited benchmark.
    \item \textbf{Custom WLAN Capture} --- Generated in a controlled lab testbed
          consisting of 3 laptops, 1 wireless access point, and 1 attacker machine
          running Ettercap. Both low-rate and high-rate ARP poisoning scenarios
          are captured over 4-hour sessions. This dataset addresses the
          WLAN-specific gap identified in the literature.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Preprocessing Steps:}
\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=2pt]
    \item Parse raw PCAP files using PyShark and extract ARP flow features.
    \item Remove records with null or infinite feature values.
    \item Cap outliers at the 99th percentile to reduce noise from burst traffic.
    \item Apply Min-Max normalization to all numerical features ($[0,1]$ range).
    \item Balance classes using SMOTE oversampling on the minority (attack) class.
    \item Split into 70\% train, 15\% validation, 15\% test (stratified by label).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

The following mathematical metrics will be used to prove success:

\begin{align}
    \text{Accuracy} &= \frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN} \\[6pt]
    \text{Precision} &= \frac{TP}{TP + FP} \\[6pt]
    \text{Recall} &= \frac{TP}{TP + FN} \\[6pt]
    \text{F1-score} &= \frac{2 \cdot \text{Precision} \cdot \text{Recall}}
                           {\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}} \\[6pt]
    \text{False Positive Rate (FPR)} &= \frac{FP}{FP + TN} \\[6pt]
    \text{AUC-ROC} &= \int_{0}^{1} TPR\, d(FPR)
\end{align}

\noindent
\textbf{Success criteria:} Detection accuracy $\geq 98\%$, F1-score $\geq 0.97$,
and FPR $\leq 2\%$ on both datasets under both low-rate and high-rate attack scenarios.

\subsection{Ethical Considerations}

\textbf{Data Privacy (GDPR Compliance).}
The CIC-IDS2017 dataset is publicly available and was generated in a controlled
lab environment containing no real user identities or personally identifiable
information. The custom WLAN dataset will be captured exclusively on a private,
isolated testbed using devices owned by the research team --- no traffic from
real users or production networks will be collected. All captured PCAPs will be
stored on encrypted storage and deleted after feature extraction. The research
therefore does not involve personal data processing and requires no GDPR consent.
Any future deployment on a live enterprise network must comply with GDPR Article~6
(lawfulness of processing) and must inform users that network traffic metadata
is being monitored.

\textbf{Algorithmic Bias.}
The custom WLAN dataset is captured in a single controlled lab environment and
may not represent the full diversity of enterprise WLAN traffic patterns.
To mitigate this, models are evaluated on both the custom dataset and the
independent CIC-IDS2017 benchmark, and per-class performance metrics are reported
to surface any class-level bias between normal and attack traffic.

\textbf{Responsible Disclosure.}
All attack simulation tools (Ettercap, ARPspoof) will be used exclusively within
the isolated lab testbed. No attacks will be performed on any real or shared network.
The research findings are intended to assist network defenders, not to enable attackers.

\textbf{Environmental Impact.}
GBM training is computationally efficient compared to deep learning alternatives
and can be completed on a standard laptop CPU without GPU acceleration. Estimated
energy consumption will be measured using the \texttt{codecarbon} Python library
and reported transparently in the final paper.
